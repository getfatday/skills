# Gene Kim Principles

## 1. The Three Ways
Flow: optimize the entire system, not local silos. Feedback: shorten and amplify all feedback loops, fail fast. Continuous Learning: foster experimentation and learning from failure.
**When to apply:** Any process improvement, organizational design, tool selection.

## 2. Unplanned Work Is the Silent Killer
Firefighting steals capacity from planned work and creates more unplanned work. It's a downward spiral. The first step is making it visible and measuring it.
**When to apply:** Capacity planning, sprint disruptions, incident management.

## 3. Make Work Visible
If you can't see the work, you can't manage the flow. Kanban boards, WIP limits, value stream maps. Invisible work is unmanageable work.
**When to apply:** Team formation, process design, tooling decisions.

## 4. Improving Daily Work > Doing Daily Work
The Third Ideal. Meta-work (improving the process, fixing tooling, reducing friction) has higher leverage than direct work. A 10% improvement in the pipeline saves more than a 10% increase in throughput.
**When to apply:** Sprint planning, investment decisions, tech debt prioritization.

## 5. Locality and Simplicity
Decompose systems and organizations so teams can work independently. Complexity kills productivity. If changing one thing requires coordinating with five teams, the system is wired wrong.
**When to apply:** Architecture decisions, team structure, microservice boundaries.

## 6. Psychological Safety
People must feel safe to fail, experiment, and speak up. Without psychological safety, no learning, no improvement, no innovation. Blameless postmortems are the practice, not just the aspiration.
**When to apply:** Incident response, team culture, retrospectives.

## 7. If It Hurts, Do It More Frequently
Deploy, integrate, test continuously. The pain of infrequent releases grows exponentially with batch size. Bring the pain forward by doing it constantly until it doesn't hurt.
**When to apply:** Release cadence, testing strategy, integration practices.

## 8. Focus on the Constraint
Any improvement made anywhere besides the bottleneck is an illusion. Identify the constraint, exploit it, subordinate everything else to it. Directly from Goldratt.
**When to apply:** Performance optimization, process improvement, capacity planning.
