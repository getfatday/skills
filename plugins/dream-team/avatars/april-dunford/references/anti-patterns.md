# April Dunford Anti-Patterns (Expert-Specific)

## Positioning by Committee
**Signal:** A room full of stakeholders debating taglines. Endless rounds of wordsmithing. No one owns the outcome.
**Dunford says:** Positioning is a cross-functional input process with a single decision-maker. Marketing facilitates, but the CEO or product leader owns the call.
**Correction:** Run a structured positioning exercise with the cross-functional team. Gather input. One leader decides.

## Feature-Led Positioning
**Signal:** Homepage leads with "AI-powered, cloud-native, real-time analytics platform." Product pages list capabilities. No mention of what the buyer actually gets.
**Dunford says:** Features are not value. Customers don't care about your architecture. They care about what it lets them do that they couldn't do before.
**Correction:** Map every differentiated capability to a customer outcome. Lead with value. Features are proof points.

## All-Things-to-All-People
**Signal:** "Our product is for any company that uses data." Positioning is so broad it could apply to 50 products. Best-fit customers are undefined.
**Dunford says:** You can't be all things to all people. Trying to appeal to everyone means your positioning resonates with no one. The best-fit customers are the ones who care desperately about your differentiated value.
**Correction:** Identify your best-fit customer segment. Position tightly for them. Let them pull you into adjacent segments.

## Skipping Competitive Alternatives
**Signal:** Team positions the product without considering what buyers compare it to. "We don't really have competitors." Differentiation is asserted, not demonstrated.
**Dunford says:** Everything is positioned relative to something. If you don't define your competitive alternatives, the buyer will, and they'll probably get it wrong.
**Correction:** Start the positioning exercise with "What would our best customers do if we didn't exist?" That's your real competitive set.

## Inventing a Category You Can't Afford
**Signal:** Startup declares they've created a new category. No analyst coverage, no search volume, no buyer vocabulary for it. Sales team spends every call explaining what the category means.
**Dunford says:** Category creation is a viable strategy if you have the budget to educate the market. Most startups don't. Pick an existing category and reframe it.
**Correction:** Position in a known category where your differentiated value stands out. Reframe if needed. Only create a category with serious investment.

## Treating Positioning as Marketing's Job
**Signal:** CEO says "Marketing needs to fix the positioning." Sales has a different pitch. Product roadmap doesn't reflect the positioning. CS tells a third story.
**Dunford says:** Positioning is a business strategy decision, not a marketing exercise. It affects sales conversations, product priorities, pricing, and customer success. Doing it in marketing alone guarantees misalignment.
**Correction:** Run positioning as a cross-functional exercise. Sales, product, CS, and marketing in the room. Align on competitive alternatives, value, and best-fit customers together.

## Set-and-Forget Positioning
**Signal:** Company positioned the product at launch three years ago. Market has shifted. Two new competitors entered. Positioning hasn't changed. Win rates are dropping and nobody connects it to positioning.
**Dunford says:** Positioning is not a tattoo. Markets move. Competitors ship new things. Your product evolves. Positioning that was right 18 months ago may be wrong today.
**Correction:** Revisit positioning when win rates shift, new competitors emerge, or the product changes significantly. Treat it as a living strategic decision.
