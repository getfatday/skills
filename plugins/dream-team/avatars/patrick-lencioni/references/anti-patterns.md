# Patrick Lencioni Anti-Patterns (Expert-Specific)

## Artificial Harmony
**Signal:** Meetings are polite, brief, and boring. No one pushes back. Real opinions surface in hallway conversations or Slack DMs after the meeting.
**Lencioni says:** "If people don't weigh in, they can't buy in." Teams that avoid conflict end up with passive sabotage disguised as agreement.
**Correction:** Mine for conflict. The leader's job is to draw out disagreements in real time. Make it safe to fight about ideas. Discomfort during the meeting is better than politics after.

## Invulnerability
**Signal:** Leaders project confidence on everything. Nobody says "I was wrong" or "I need help." Mistakes get buried or blamed on circumstances.
**Lencioni says:** Trust is built on vulnerability. If the leader won't go first, the team never will. A leader who can't say "I screwed up" creates a team that hides problems.
**Correction:** The leader goes first. Share a failure, ask for feedback, admit uncertainty. Vulnerability is not weakness. It's the foundation of trust.

## Inattention to Results
**Signal:** Team members care more about their department's success, their personal career, or their status than the team's collective outcomes. People hoard resources and protect turf.
**Lencioni says:** "The ultimate dysfunction of a team is the tendency of members to care about something other than the collective goals of the group."
**Correction:** Define shared results publicly. Make collective outcomes the scoreboard. Reward team results, not individual heroics.

## Smart Organization Over Healthy Organization
**Signal:** Leadership invests in strategy consultants, technology upgrades, and talent acquisition while ignoring politics, low morale, and confusion about priorities.
**Lencioni says:** "The single greatest advantage any company can achieve is organizational health. Yet it is ignored by most leaders because they think it's beneath them or too soft."
**Correction:** Treat organizational health as the multiplier. A healthy organization makes smart decisions stick. An unhealthy one undermines even brilliant strategy.

## Death by Meeting
**Signal:** Meetings blend strategic, tactical, and administrative topics into one session. They run long, accomplish little, and everyone dreads them. Or the opposite: meetings get eliminated entirely.
**Lencioni says:** Bad meetings are a symptom of a lack of contextual structure. The problem isn't too many meetings. It's wrong meetings.
**Correction:** Four meeting types, each with a distinct purpose. Daily check-in (5 minutes, standing). Weekly tactical (45-90 minutes, metrics and obstacles). Monthly strategic (2-4 hours, one or two big topics). Quarterly offsite (1-2 days, review and reset).

## Ambiguity as a Management Strategy
**Signal:** Leaders avoid making clear decisions so they can't be blamed if things go wrong. Priorities stay vague. "We're focused on everything." Team members interpret direction differently.
**Lencioni says:** Commitment requires clarity. If people leave a meeting with different interpretations of what was decided, there was no decision. Ambiguity breeds politics.
**Correction:** End every meeting by reviewing what was decided and who owns it. Overcommunicate the answers. Repetition is not a sign of poor communication. It's the only way to ensure alignment.

## Hiring Without the Three Virtues
**Signal:** Hiring focuses on skills and experience but ignores character. The team ends up with skilled people who are arrogant, lazy, or politically manipulative.
**Lencioni says:** Someone who is humble and hungry but not smart is an "accidental mess-maker." Someone who is hungry and smart but not humble is a "skillful politician." All three virtues are required.
**Correction:** Screen for humble, hungry, and smart with the same rigor as technical skills. One missing virtue creates a distinct and predictable problem on the team.
