# Anti-Patterns

Patterns that erode trust, courage, and belonging in teams. Each is a form of armor that feels protective but blocks the vulnerability required for real connection and innovation.

## Armored Leadership

Protecting yourself instead of connecting with your team. Using positional authority, expertise, or emotional distance as a shield. Armored leaders control information, avoid hard conversations, and signal that vulnerability is not welcome here.

**What it looks like:** A leader who never says "I don't know." Who responds to questions with credentials instead of curiosity. Who keeps a professional mask on so tight that no one on the team knows if they are struggling, learning, or fully checked out.

**The cost:** Teams mirror the armor. If you are not willing to be vulnerable, you are asking your team to do something you will not do yourself. Innovation dies because no one will risk being wrong.

**The shift:** Daring leadership. Show up without the armor. Say "I don't know, but let's figure it out." Model the behavior you want to see. Strong back, soft front, wild heart.

## Shame-Based Management

Using blame, humiliation, put-downs, or public callouts to drive behavior. Shame does not motivate. It paralyzes. It drives people underground where they hide mistakes instead of surfacing them.

**What it looks like:** Calling out errors in group settings. "I can't believe you did that." Eye rolls. Sarcasm disguised as humor. Making an example of someone. Performance management through fear.

**The cost:** You get compliance, not commitment. People stop taking risks. Mistakes get buried. You lose the ability to learn fast because no one will surface what is going wrong.

**The shift:** Accountability with empathy. Separate the behavior from the person. "The behavior is the problem, not you." Hold high standards and hold people with care. These are not in conflict.

## Avoiding Difficult Conversations

Sidestepping the rumble. Choosing comfort over clarity. Telling yourself the story that you are being kind by not bringing it up, when you are actually being unkind by letting it fester.

**What it looks like:** "It's fine." Back-channeling complaints to other people instead of going direct. Hinting instead of saying it plainly. Letting performance issues slide for months. Giving vague feedback that protects your comfort but leaves the other person confused.

**The cost:** Resentment builds. The story-making machine fills in every gap with the worst-case scenario. Small issues become relationship-ending conflicts because they were never addressed when they were small.

**The shift:** Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind. Skill up on hard conversations. Use "the story I'm making up" to open dialogue without accusation. Lean in to the discomfort. It is temporary. Avoidance is permanent.

## Perfectionism Masking Fear

Dressing up fear of failure and fear of judgment as high standards. Perfectionism is not about striving for excellence. It is a twenty-ton shield that we carry around thinking it will protect us when it is actually the thing that is preventing us from being seen.

**What it looks like:** Endless iteration without shipping. "It's not ready yet." Rewriting the deck for the fifth time. Holding work back because it might be criticized. Setting standards so high that no one, including yourself, can meet them.

**The cost:** Nothing ships. Teams burn out chasing an impossible bar. People internalize the message that they are not enough. Healthy striving gets replaced by fear-driven grinding.

**The shift:** Healthy striving is self-focused: "How can I improve?" Perfectionism is other-focused: "What will they think?" Name the fear. Ship the work. Get in the arena.

## Cynicism as Self-Protection

Mocking vulnerability, dismissing emotional conversations, and calling it being realistic. Cynicism is armor. It lets you stay above the fray without ever having to risk anything.

**What it looks like:** Eye rolls when someone shares something personal. "That's just corporate feel-good stuff." Preemptive criticism of new ideas. "That'll never work here." Treating enthusiasm as naivete.

**The cost:** Belonging dies. People stop bringing their full selves. Creativity requires vulnerability, and cynicism is a vulnerability killer. You end up with a team of armored professionals going through the motions.

**The shift:** Curiosity over cynicism. "Tell me more about that." Engage before you critique. If you are not in the arena, your criticism is not data.

## Fitting-In Culture

Requiring conformity and calling it culture. Fitting in is the opposite of belonging. Fitting in says: change who you are to be accepted here. Belonging says: show up as you are and we will hold space for it.

**What it looks like:** Unwritten rules about how to dress, speak, and emote. Penalizing people who think differently. Rewarding assimilation. "That's not how we do things here." Diversity without inclusion.

**The cost:** You lose the cognitive diversity that drives innovation. People spend energy performing instead of contributing. The people who can least afford to conform, often those from marginalized backgrounds, pay the highest price.

**The shift:** True belonging does not require you to change who you are. It requires you to be who you are. Build a culture where people can disagree, be different, and still belong.

## Gold-Plating Failure

Refusing to own what went wrong. Spinning every failure into a success story. Skipping the reckoning and jumping straight to "lessons learned" without actually sitting in the discomfort of what happened.

**What it looks like:** Post-mortems that blame external factors. "The market shifted." Reframing clear failures as strategic pivots without acknowledging the cost. Leaders who never say "I got this wrong."

**The cost:** You cannot rise strong if you skip the fall. Teams learn that failure is not safe to name, so they stop naming it. The same mistakes repeat because no one did the honest reckoning.

**The shift:** Rising strong requires three steps: the reckoning (owning the emotion), the rumble (getting honest about the story), and the revolution (rewriting the ending based on truth, not self-protection). You cannot skip steps.
