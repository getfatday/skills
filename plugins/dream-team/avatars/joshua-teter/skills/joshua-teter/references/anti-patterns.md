# Technical Program Management Anti-Patterns — Joshua Teter

## The Glorified Project Manager
**Signal:** "The TPM tracks tasks in Jira and sends weekly status emails."
**Why it fails:** That's a project coordinator, not a TPM. No technical engagement, no strategic value, no cross-functional leadership.
**Correction:** TPMs must engage technically (architecture reviews, trade-off discussions) and strategically (alignment to business outcomes).
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## The Meeting Collector
**Signal:** "My calendar is 90% meetings — I must be adding value."
**Why it fails:** Measuring value by calendar density, not outcomes. Meetings are overhead unless they produce decisions, unblock work, or mitigate risks.
**Correction:** Measure by decisions made, risks mitigated, and blockers removed. Design communication architecture deliberately — fewer meetings, more clarity.
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## Jumping to Execution
**Signal:** "Let's just start building — we'll figure out the strategy later."
**Why it fails:** Produces well-managed failures. Programs delivered on time that solve the wrong problem. Alignment debt compounds with every sprint.
**Correction:** Strategy before execution. ALIGN phase first: business context, success criteria, executive intent.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Part I

## The Heroic Individual
**Signal:** "Only Sarah can run this program — she knows everything."
**Why it fails:** Hero dependency. If the program collapses when one person goes on vacation, the mechanisms are broken.
**Correction:** Build mechanisms: documented processes, shared knowledge, clear escalation paths. The system works without any individual.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Amazon influence

## Risk Theater
**Signal:** "We have a risk register — it's in the shared drive somewhere."
**Why it fails:** Risk register as compliance checkbox. Risks identified but never mitigated, reviewed, or escalated. Theater, not management.
**Correction:** Living risk register with owners, trigger conditions, mitigation due dates, regular reviews. Risk meetings are not status meetings.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, risk chapters

## Conflating Roles
**Signal:** "We need a TPM — someone to coordinate stuff."
**Why it fails:** Using TPM as catch-all degrades the role and outcomes. PM owns what/why, EM owns people/code, TPM owns cross-team delivery, ScrumMaster owns team process.
**Correction:** Big Five TPM Comparison. Define distinct accountabilities before hiring.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Ch. 1

## Communication as Afterthought
**Signal:** "Execution is going great — we just need to update the stakeholders... eventually."
**Why it fails:** A program with perfect execution but terrible communication will be perceived as failing. Perception = reality for stakeholders.
**Correction:** Communication IS the product. Design communication architecture: audience, purpose, format, cadence, owner for each channel.
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## Technology Worship
**Signal:** "Let's adopt this new AI dashboard / project tool / automation platform."
**Why it fails:** Adopting tools without asking if they solve actual problems. Tools serve process; process serves outcomes. Shiny tools don't fix broken processes.
**Correction:** Start with the problem. What decision is not being made? What risk is not being surfaced? Then choose the tool.
**Source:** TPM Handbook 2nd edition

## Avoiding Conflict
**Signal:** "I don't want to push back on the VP — they won't like it."
**Why it fails:** TPMs who prioritize being liked over being effective. Cross-functional leadership requires navigating disagreement and delivering unwelcome news.
**Correction:** Influence without authority includes difficult conversations. Use data, use frameworks, be professional — but don't avoid the truth.
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## The Lone Wolf
**Signal:** "I'll handle all the coordination myself."
**Why it fails:** Influence without authority requires a coalition. No single person can orchestrate across 5 teams without allies, sponsors, and informed stakeholders.
**Correction:** Build your network. Identify sponsors, technical leads, and informal influencers. Relationship capital is compound interest.
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## Scope Creep Acceptance
**Signal:** "Sure, we can add that feature — no problem."
**Why it fails:** Saying yes to everything to avoid conflict. Every addition has a cost in time, resources, and risk — and that cost must be made visible.
**Correction:** Rigorous scope management. Every change request gets impact analysis: what does this cost, what does it delay, what risks does it add?
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## Status Quo Bias
**Signal:** "We've always done standups on Monday at 9am — that's just how we work."
**Why it fails:** Processes that serve tradition, not outcomes. Cadences, formats, and tools should be regularly evaluated.
**Correction:** Retrospect on process, not just product. Does this meeting still serve its purpose? Is this report still read?
**Source:** TPM Handbook
