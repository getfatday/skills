# Technical Program Management Principles — Joshua Teter

## 1. TPMs Are the Connective Tissue
TPMs don't own the product vision or the code. They own the system of delivery — binding teams, systems, and strategy. The nervous system of an engineering organization.

**When to apply:** Role definition, org design, when a TPM's scope is questioned.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, both editions

## 2. Influence Without Authority
Lead through persuasion, data, and relationship capital. Positional power is not available. Build credibility by demonstrating technical depth, delivering consistently, and maintaining relationships.

**When to apply:** Every stakeholder interaction. Every cross-team negotiation.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, stakeholder management chapters

## 3. Technical Depth Is Non-Negotiable
A TPM who cannot read code, understand architecture, or engage in technical trade-off discussions is a project manager with a fancy title. Technical depth earns credibility; credibility enables influence.

**When to apply:** Hiring, self-assessment, when a TPM is excluded from technical discussions.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, LinkedIn posts

## 4. Programs Are Not Big Projects
A program is a collection of interdependent projects aligned to a strategic outcome. Treating a program as a big project is a category error that leads to micromanagement and missed cross-cutting risks.

**When to apply:** Program scoping, when someone asks you to "manage this project" and hands you 5 teams.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Ch. 1-2

## 5. Risk Management Is Proactive, Not Reactive
The best TPMs surface risks before they become issues. Define trigger conditions upfront. Maintain a living risk register with owners, mitigations, and escalation paths. Regular risk reviews, not status meetings.

**When to apply:** Program setup, weekly cadence, when "everything is green" feels too comfortable.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, risk management chapters

## 6. Communication Is the Product
The TPM's primary deliverable is clarity. Clear status, clear risks, clear decisions needed, clear ownership. A program with perfect execution but terrible communication will be perceived as failing.

**When to apply:** Every status update, every executive review, every cross-team sync.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, LinkedIn

## 7. Strategy Before Execution
Understand the "why" before building the "how." Jumping to execution without strategic alignment produces well-managed failures — programs delivered on time that solve the wrong problem.

**When to apply:** Program kickoff, when pressure to "just start building" is high.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Part I

## 8. Stakeholder Management Is Relationship Engineering
Map stakeholders by power, interest, and disposition. Define relationship strategies per quadrant. High-power blockers get the most attention. Revisit the map at every phase transition.

**When to apply:** Program setup, when encountering resistance, after organizational changes.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, stakeholder chapters

## 9. The TPM Role Is Distinct
TPM ≠ PM ≠ EM ≠ ScrumMaster ≠ non-technical Program Manager. Each has distinct accountability, decision rights, technical depth, and scope. Conflating them degrades all.

**When to apply:** Hiring, org design, when "TPM" is used as a catch-all title.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Ch. 1; Big Five TPM Comparison

## 10. Emotional Intelligence Multiplies Technical Intelligence
EI is not a soft skill — it's a force multiplier for every hard skill. Stakeholder negotiation, conflict resolution, team morale, executive communication, cultural navigation. AI handles data; EI handles humans.

**When to apply:** Conflict, executive communication, team dynamics, when pure logic isn't landing.
**Source:** TPM Handbook 2nd edition, AI/EI Integration Model

## 11. Mechanisms Over Heroics
Build repeatable, scalable processes that produce consistent outcomes without depending on individuals. If the program falls apart when you go on vacation, you've failed at building mechanisms.

**When to apply:** Process design, when someone is the single point of failure, post-incident reviews.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, Amazon cultural influence

## 12. Data-Driven Decisions, Narrative-Driven Communication
Decide with data. Communicate with stories. Numbers convince the analytical; narratives move organizations. The TPM lives at the intersection.

**When to apply:** Executive reviews, team alignment, when data alone isn't creating urgency.
**Source:** TPM Handbook

## 13. Ambiguity Is the TPM's Natural Habitat
If everything were clear, you wouldn't need a TPM. Comfort with ambiguity and the ability to create structure from chaos is definitional. Don't wait for clarity — create it.

**When to apply:** Program inception, when requirements are vague, when teams are uncertain.
**Source:** TPM Handbook, podcast appearances
