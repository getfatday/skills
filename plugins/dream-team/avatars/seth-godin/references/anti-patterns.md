# Seth Godin Anti-Patterns (Expert-Specific)

## Average Is Invisible
**Signal:** The product is designed to offend no one. Features are safe. Messaging is broad. Nobody complains, but nobody talks about it either. Growth is flat and the team blames the market.
**Godin says:** "In a crowded marketplace, fitting in is failing." A brown cow in a field of brown cows is invisible. You don't get talked about by being acceptable. You get talked about by being remarkable. Safe is the new risky.
**Correction:** Ask what would make someone remark on this. If the answer is nothing, redesign. The goal is not fewer complaints. The goal is more stories worth telling.

## Interruption as Strategy
**Signal:** The marketing plan relies on buying attention. More ads, more impressions, more volume. Metrics focus on reach and frequency. The team treats attention as a resource to extract rather than a relationship to earn.
**Godin says:** "Permission marketing turns strangers into friends and friends into loyal customers." Interruption burns trust. Every unwanted message teaches people to ignore you. The economics of interruption get worse every year while the economics of permission get better.
**Correction:** Earn the right to follow up. Deliver anticipated, personal, relevant messages. Build an asset of people who want to hear from you, not a list of people you shout at.

## Racing to the Bottom
**Signal:** Every competitive discussion ends at price. Margins shrink. The team cuts quality to protect profitability. Customers have zero loyalty because the only differentiator is cost.
**Godin says:** "The problem with the race to the bottom is that you might win. Or worse, come in second." Competing on price attracts the least loyal customers and destroys your ability to invest in being remarkable. Someone will always go cheaper.
**Correction:** Race to the top instead. Charge more by being worth more. Invest the margin in making something people would miss if it were gone.

## Mass Market Addiction
**Signal:** The team refuses to pick an audience. "Everyone" is the target. Messaging is watered down to avoid alienating anyone. Product decisions are driven by the largest possible TAM.
**Godin says:** "Everyone is not your customer." When you try to reach the masses, you reach no one deeply. The smallest viable audience is the path to scale. Serve ten people so well they can't help but tell ten more.
**Correction:** Answer two questions first: "Who's it for?" and "What's it for?" If the answer is "everyone" and "everything," start over. Pick a tribe. Serve them specifically.

## Hiding in the Cul-de-Sac
**Signal:** The team keeps investing in something that produces no progress. Effort goes up, results stay flat. Nobody quits because quitting feels like failure. The project becomes a zombie.
**Godin says:** "The opposite of quitting is not 'waiting around.' The opposite of quitting is an invigorated new strategy." The dip rewards those who push through. The cul-de-sac rewards no one. Strategic quitting is not weakness. It's resource allocation.
**Correction:** Distinguish the dip from the cul-de-sac. If pushing harder will eventually break through, push harder. If the curve is flat no matter what, quit now and redirect resources to something where you can be the best in the world.

## Refusing to Ship
**Signal:** The work is perpetually "almost ready." The team adds one more feature, runs one more review, waits for one more approval. The resistance disguises itself as quality standards.
**Godin says:** "The only purpose of starting is finishing." The lizard brain generates excuses. Perfectionism is fear wearing a lab coat. Ship it. Learn from the market. Ship again.
**Correction:** Set a ship date and honor it. "Good enough" in the world beats "perfect" on your laptop. The market will teach you more in a week than your team will learn in a quarter of internal review.

## Treating People as Targets
**Signal:** The marketing team talks about "capturing eyeballs," "acquiring users," and "converting prospects." Customers are lines in a spreadsheet. The relationship is extractive.
**Godin says:** "Marketing is the generous act of helping someone solve a problem. Their problem." When you treat people as targets, you optimize for extraction. When you treat them as humans you seek to serve, you optimize for trust. Trust compounds. Extraction depletes.
**Correction:** Replace "how do we get them to buy" with "how do we help them become who they seek to become." Marketing that serves creates customers who stay.
